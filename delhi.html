Red Fort:

The Red Fort, also known as Lal Qila, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and an iconic symbol of Delhi's rich Mughal heritage. It was built by Emperor Shah Jahan in the 17th century as the fortified palace of the Mughal Empire. The fort's imposing red sandstone walls enclose a complex of palaces, gardens, and pavilions, including the Diwan-i-Aam (Hall of Public Audience) and the Diwan-i-Khas (Hall of Private Audience). The Red Fort served as the center of Mughal power for centuries and continues to be a major tourist attraction and cultural landmark in Delhi.

Qutub Minar:

Qutub Minar is the tallest brick minaret in the world and another UNESCO World Heritage Site in Delhi. It was built in the 12th century by Qutb-ud-din Aibak, the founder of the Delhi Sultanate, and later completed by his successors. The minaret stands at a height of 73 meters and is adorned with intricate carvings and verses from the Quran. Surrounding the Qutub Minar is the Qutub Complex, which includes several other historic structures, such as the Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque, Alai Darwaza, and Iron Pillar of Delhi.

Humayun's Tomb:

Humayun's Tomb is a majestic mausoleum and UNESCO World Heritage Site built in the 16th century for Emperor Humayun, the second Mughal emperor of India. It is considered a precursor to the architectural style of the Taj Mahal and is renowned for its symmetrical design, Persian-inspired architecture, and lush gardens. Humayun's Tomb is surrounded by a charbagh (four-part) garden layout and features a central dome and intricate marble lattice work. The tomb complex also houses the graves of several other Mughal rulers and their family members.

India Gate:

India Gate is a war memorial and iconic landmark located in the heart of Delhi. It was built in the early 20th century to commemorate the soldiers of the British Indian Army who lost their lives in World War I and the Third Anglo-Afghan War. Designed by Sir Edwin Lutyens, India Gate stands at a height of 42 meters and is surrounded by lush lawns and gardens. It serves as a popular gathering spot for locals and tourists, especially in the evenings when the monument is illuminated.

Jama Masjid:

Jama Masjid is one of the largest and most revered mosques in India, located in the bustling area of Old Delhi. It was built by Emperor Shah Jahan between 1644 and 1656 and is known for its imposing red sandstone and white marble architecture. Jama Masjid can accommodate up to 25,000 worshippers at a time and is adorned with three massive domes, minarets, and intricately designed prayer halls. The mosque is a significant religious and cultural landmark in Delhi and attracts devotees and tourists from all over the world.

Lotus Temple:

The Lotus Temple, also known as the Bahá'í House of Worship, is a distinctive architectural marvel located in South Delhi. It was completed in 1986 and is shaped like a lotus flower, with 27 marble-clad "petals" arranged in clusters. The Lotus Temple is a symbol of the Bahá'í Faith's emphasis on unity, peace, and harmony among all religions and people. Visitors of all backgrounds are welcome to meditate, reflect, and pray in the tranquil atmosphere of the temple, which is surrounded by lush gardens and pools.

Akshardham Temple:

Akshardham Temple is a magnificent Hindu temple complex located on the banks of the Yamuna River in East Delhi. It was inaugurated in 2005 and is dedicated to Bhagwan Swaminarayan, a revered saint and spiritual leader. Akshardham Temple is renowned for its intricate carvings, sculptures, and architectural grandeur, which showcase thousands of years of Indian culture, art, and spirituality. The complex also features exhibitions, musical fountain shows, and a sprawling garden, making it a popular tourist destination and spiritual retreat.

Chandni Chowk:

Chandni Chowk is one of the oldest and busiest markets in Delhi, located in the historic area of Old Delhi. It was built by Emperor Shah Jahan in the 17th century as a grand bazaar and commercial center. Chandni Chowk is known for its narrow lanes, bustling streets, and vibrant atmosphere, offering a fascinating blend of shops, street food stalls, spice markets, and historical landmarks. Visitors can explore the market's diverse offerings, including traditional textiles, jewelry, handicrafts, and delectable Indian cuisine.

Gurudwara Bangla Sahib:

Gurudwara Bangla Sahib is one of the most prominent Sikh gurdwaras in Delhi, located near Connaught Place. It was originally a bungalow belonging to Raja Jai Singh, which was later converted into a gurdwara to commemorate the visit of Guru Harkrishan Sahib, the eighth Sikh Guru. Gurudwara Bangla Sahib is known for its serene atmosphere, sarovar (holy pond), and langar (community kitchen) that serves free meals to visitors regardless of their religion or background.

National Museum:

The National Museum is one of the largest museums in India and is located in the heart of Delhi, near India Gate. It was established in 1949 and showcases a vast collection of artifacts, artworks, and historical objects representing India's rich cultural heritage and history. The museum's exhibits include sculptures, paintings, textiles, manuscripts, and archaeological treasures spanning over 5,000 years of Indian civilization.

Raj Ghat:

Raj Ghat is a memorial dedicated to Mahatma Gandhi, the Father of the Nation, located on the banks of the Yamuna River. It marks the spot where Gandhi was cremated following his assassination in 1948. Raj Ghat is a simple black marble platform surrounded by lush gardens and is a place of reverence and reflection for millions of people who visit to pay their respects to the Mahatma.
